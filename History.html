History

For the longest time foxes were seen as untamable wild animals, and while modern domesticated foxes are not quite on the same level as dogs and cats they have still made immense progress in the not so distant past.

While the domestication of the wild fox had been in part started before the mid 20th century it is mainly agreed that the start of what we have today came from the experiment done in 1959 by the geneticist Dimitri Belyaev. Belyaev had read Charles Darwin’s studies on the domestic traits of animals and this piqued his interest. Belyaev was interested in how the domestication of wolves from dogs happened and based his experiment on that. The key difference was that in his experiment he used the silver fox population of Russia as the test subjects.

In the 1930s Belyaev was a student in Moscow at the Ivanova Agricultural Academy. After his graduation and service in WWII Belyaev was able to get a job at the Institute for Fur Breeding Animals. After some time and experience with the animals at the institute as well as some time at Ivanova Agricultural Academy he had a better understanding of the traits of domesticated animals. Physical traits included patterns of fur with spots or streaks of color, short and/or curly tails, floppy and/or big ears, as well as an overall more juvenile appearance to their wild counterparts. There were also differences in behavior. Domesticated animals, while obviously being more friendly towards humans, also exhibited reduced stress and hormone levels as well as a longer breeding season. These traits are now known as the domestication syndrome.

Belyaev found it interesting that regardless of what humans chose certain animals for they all ended up exhibiting these traits in one way or another. He made the conclusion that this process must start with people choosing the animals that are reacting more positively to humans to begin with. Belyaev hypothesized that these traits that were found in domestic animals must in some way be initially linked with the animals that are already more tame to begin with. So using this he began his experiment.

In 1959 Belyaev decided to do this experiment with an animal that he had already spent much time with at the institut. That being the silver fox. With each generation of foxes Belyaev and his team would select the 10% of foxes they deemed to be most tame and would use them to breed the next generation. Using a scale they developed for measuring tameness the research team could continue to select groups of foxes and see if the following generation exhibited more traits from the domestication syndrome. As the project was underway Belyaev died in 1985 but Lyudmila Trut, a member of his team who had become the lead researcher, continued the experiment. Trut was around 25 at this time and in the time since major findings have been found in the experiment.

The experiment has been going on for nearly 6 decades and the resulting foxes today are nothing short of amazing. The foxes selected exclusively for their rating on the tameness scale have produced an amazing selection of domesticated foxes. These foxes will lick people's hands, can be picked up and petted, will wag their tails while humans are present and will even whine when humans leave them. The stress levels of these animals are about half of the levels exhibited from the first generation. Likewise, their serotonin levels are much higher, meaning they are noticeably happier. Similarly, these foxes exhibit the physical traits of domestication such as floppy ears, curly tails, rounder snouts, more juvenile bodies, and dog-like fur patterns. Another surprising change found is that, differently from wild foxes, these foxes are able to follow the “human gaze” in the same way dogs do. In the early days of the experiment the foxes made up a small percentage of the group. Today they make up nearly the entire group. Interestingly, a similar experiment was started not long after this one in which foxes were bred for more aggressive traits. These foxes did not exhibit the traits of domestication.

Overall, this experiment has been able to prove Belyaev’s hypothesis correct. Animals selected on tameness are inclined to become much more domesticated than the average fox even without exposure to humans. A result of this experiment is a wide range of domestic foxes in the modern day.
